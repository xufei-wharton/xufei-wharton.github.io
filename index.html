About me

Hello! I am a fifth-year PhD candidate in Operations Management at The Wharton School, University of Pennsylvania, advised by Gad Allon and Ken Moon.

I study what AI and monitoring technologies actually do to the people who use them. Most of what we know about how new technology feels to use comes from surveys taken after the fact. I instrument the worker, not just the workflow: in my experiments, people work with LLM assistants while sensors read workload directly off the body — heart rate, skin conductance, pupil dilation, eye movements, every keystroke — the kind of telemetry usually reserved for fighter pilots and athletes.

My job market paper began with a puzzle. We gave practicing clinicians LLM drafts to ease their documentation work, and on average the drafts saved no time at all — clinicians are accountable for what goes in the chart, so minutes saved writing became minutes spent verifying and editing. In the lab, drafts did deliver: writers were about 60% more productive and said the work felt lighter. But their bodies told a different story, with fatigue accumulating just as fast as when writing from scratch. Much of my research lives in that gap between how work feels and what it actually costs — and in designing when-to-intervene policies that keep technology-assisted work sustainable.

Chasing these questions has taken me from clinical documentation to U.S. Air Force training flights to a language-learning platform handling six million reviews a month, with methods spanning lab and field experiments, decision-focused machine learning, structural estimation, and reinforcement learning.

Dissertation committee: Gad Allon, Ken Moon, Hummy Song, and Christian Terwiesch.

Before Wharton, I received a B.S. in Industrial and Systems Engineering from the Georgia Institute of Technology (Honors Program).
